## Part A – Two Professional Emails

### Email 1 — Student to Professor (Deadline Extension Request)

**Purpose of the Email:**
To formally request my professor for a short extension on the
CSE0001 Digital Literacy Project Assignment submission deadline
due to a genuine reason.

**Structure I Followed:**

| Email Element | What I Wrote |
|---------------|--------------|
| To | professor.name@vitbhopal.ac.in |
| From | roneet.sarode2025@vitbhopal.ac.in |
| Subject Line | Request for Extension of Assignment Submission Deadline – CSE0001 |
| Greeting | Dear Professor [Last Name], |
| Introduction | Introduced myself with name, reg no., branch, and course |
| Body | Explained the reason politely, mentioned original and new date |
| Apology | Apologized for inconvenience caused |
| Assurance | Promised quality submission by the requested new date |
| Sign-off | Yours sincerely, with full name and reg number |

**Key Rules I Followed:**
- Used a clear and specific subject line
- Addressed the professor formally and respectfully
- Did not over-explain or give too many personal details
- Kept the tone humble, polite, and professional
- Included all my identification details in the sign-off

**What I Learned:**
Writing a formal email to a professor is very different from
sending a casual WhatsApp message. Every word matters — the
subject line, the greeting, the tone, and even the sign-off.
A well-written email shows respect for the reader's time and
creates a positive impression even in a difficult situation
like requesting an extension.

### Email 2 — Student to Internship Coordinator

**Purpose of the Email:**
To express my interest in a summer internship opportunity at a
company working in the field of Artificial Intelligence or
technology.

**Structure I Followed:**

| Email Element | What I Wrote |
|---------------|--------------|
| To | internships@companyname.com |
| From | roneet.sarode2025@vitbhopal.ac.in |
| Subject Line | Application for Summer Internship – Artificial Intelligence / Roneet Sarode |
| Greeting | Dear Sir/Madam, |
| Introduction | Name, reg no., branch, institution clearly stated |
| Body Para 1 | Expressed genuine interest in internship at the company |
| Body Para 2 | Highlighted Python skills, GitHub, Kaggle, HackerRank activity |
| Body Para 3 | Connected my AI interest with the company's domain |
| Closing | Offered to share GitHub profile and academic records |
| Sign-off | Yours sincerely, with GitHub and LinkedIn links |

**Key Rules I Followed:**
- Subject line includes my name and purpose — easy to identify
- Used "Dear Sir/Madam" when the recipient's name is unknown
- Highlighted relevant skills without being overconfident
- Kept the email concise — 4 short paragraphs only
- Included GitHub and LinkedIn links to show digital presence

**What I Learned:**
An internship email is essentially a first impression with a
company. I learned that the subject line alone can determine
whether the email gets opened or ignored. Including specific
skills and platforms like GitHub shows that I am already
building a professional presence, which makes the email stand
out from generic requests.

**File Location:** Saved as `email2-internship.md` in
task-4-email-etiquette/ folder.

## Part B – Social Media Do's and Don'ts Checklist

**What I Created:**
A detailed checklist of 7 Do's and 7 Don'ts for responsible
social media use as a college student. The checklist is designed
to be practical and directly relevant to students like me who
are just beginning their digital journey.

**Summary of Key Points:**

✅ TOP 5 DO'S:
1. Keep LinkedIn and GitHub profiles updated regularly
2. Use a professional profile photo on LinkedIn
3. Share academic achievements and projects online
4. Think before posting — "Would I show this to my professor?"
5. Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) on all accounts

❌ TOP 5 DON'TS:
1. Never share passwords, OTPs, or personal details online
2. Do not post offensive or inappropriate content
3. Never share fake news or unverified information
4. Do not accept requests from unknown strangers
5. Never post your home address or daily routine publicly

## 📖 Reflection 

For Task 4, I drafted two professional emails and created a
Social Media Do's and Don'ts checklist. One real situation
where poor digital communication caused a serious problem is
the case of college students posting negative and offensive
comments about their faculty and institution on public social
media platforms. Screenshots of these posts were shared widely
and eventually reached the college administration, resulting
in disciplinary action against those students. These posts,
made in a moment of frustration, permanently damaged their
academic reputation and future opportunities.

What could have been done differently is that the students
should have raised their concerns through proper official
channels — such as writing a formal email to the concerned
faculty or using the institution's official feedback system.
This incident taught me that anything shared online is
permanent and public, and that even students must practice
responsible and professional digital communication at all
times.


## 💡 Key Learnings from Task 4

1. A professional email has a specific structure that must
   always be followed — subject, greeting, body, sign-off.

2. The subject line is the most important part of any email
   — it decides whether the email gets read or ignored.

3. Tone matters more than length — a short, well-written
   email is always better than a long, poorly structured one.

4. Social media posts are permanent — even deleted posts can
   be screenshotted and shared by others.

5. As an AI student, my GitHub and LinkedIn profiles are my
   most important digital communication tools — they speak
   for me even when I am not in the room.
